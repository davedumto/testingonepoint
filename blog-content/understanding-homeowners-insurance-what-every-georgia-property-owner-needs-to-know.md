## What a Standard HO-3 Policy Covers

The HO-3 is the most common homeowners form in Georgia. It covers your home on an open-perils basis and your belongings on a named-perils basis. Knowing how the six core coverages work is the difference between being protected and being surprised at claim time.

### Six core coverages

- **Dwelling (Coverage A)** — the structure of your home, including attached garage, built-ins, and foundation

- **Other Structures (Coverage B)** — detached garages, sheds, fences, and driveways

- **Personal Property (Coverage C)** — furniture, clothing, electronics, and everything else inside your home

- **Loss of Use (Coverage D)** — hotel, food, and additional living expenses while your home is unlivable

- **Personal Liability (Coverage E)** — lawsuits for bodily injury or property damage you cause

- **Medical Payments (Coverage F)** — no-fault medical bills for guests hurt on your property

### What the limits mean

- **Coverage A** should equal the full rebuild cost of your home — not the market value and not the tax appraisal

- **Coverage B** is usually automatically set at 10% of Coverage A

- **Coverage C** is typically 50–75% of Coverage A by default, though you can raise or lower it

## Georgia-Specific Risks Your Policy Should Address

Georgia's geography creates a wider range of natural hazards than most states. A policy that works in Minnesota is not the right policy for Savannah — or for Blue Ridge.

- **Tropical storms and hurricanes** — southeast Georgia and the coast face named-storm wind and surge risk

- **Severe thunderstorms and hail** — statewide, and a leading cause of roof claims every spring

- **Tornadoes** — North Georgia sees an active tornado season from March through May

- **Ice storms** — North Georgia winters can drop branches on roofs and knock out power for days

- **Wildfire** — drought years elevate risk for rural and wooded properties

- **Flash flooding** — Georgia's hard clay soil sheds water fast, flooding basements and low-lying lots

## What Homeowners Insurance Does NOT Cover

Most Georgia homeowners are shocked to learn what their policy excludes by default. These are the most common gaps — and every one of them is a regular cause of uncovered claims.

- **Flood damage** — requires a separate NFIP or private flood policy

- **Earthquake damage** — rare in Georgia but not zero; requires an endorsement

- **Normal wear and tear** — insurance pays for sudden damage, not deferred maintenance

- **Sewer and drain backup** — needs a specific endorsement, often $25–$75/year

- **Mold** — usually capped at a small sublimit unless you add a mold rider

- **Damage from neglect** — a slow leak you ignored is not a covered loss

- **Home-based business inventory** — covered only up to a small sublimit; a business endorsement or BOP fills the gap

## Replacement Cost vs. Actual Cash Value

This is the single most important decision on your policy, and most homeowners never realize they made it. The difference between Replacement Cost Value (RCV) and Actual Cash Value (ACV) is the difference between a new roof and a check for a 15-year-old depreciated one.

- **Replacement Cost Value (RCV)** — pays what it costs today to replace the damaged item with new equivalent

- **Actual Cash Value (ACV)** — pays replacement cost minus depreciation for age and wear

We strongly recommend **RCV on both dwelling and contents**. The premium difference is small — often $10–$20/month — but the payout gap after a claim can be tens of thousands of dollars.

## How Your Georgia Premium Is Calculated

Two houses on the same street can pay dramatically different rates. Carriers weigh these factors most heavily:

- **Home age and construction** — older Atlanta homes with knob-and-tube or polybutylene plumbing cost more

- **ZIP code** — Fulton and DeKalb urban areas price differently than coastal Chatham County

- **Claims history (CLUE report)** — the last 5–7 years of claims on the home and on you personally

- **Deductible** — raising from $1,000 to $2,500 can cut premium 10–15%

- **Roof age and material** — roofs over 15 years old often trigger surcharges or ACV-only settlement

- **Credit-based insurance score** — legal in Georgia and a significant rating factor

- **Bundling with auto** — multi-policy discounts often save 15–25%

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Does Georgia require homeowners insurance?

Technically no — Georgia law doesn't mandate homeowners insurance. But lenders always require it for mortgaged homes, and even if you own your home outright, going without is a huge financial gamble on your single largest asset.

### What is a wind/hail deductible?

A separate, percentage-based deductible (usually 1–5% of dwelling coverage) that only triggers for named storms or hail events. It's common in coastal and storm-prone Georgia ZIP codes. On a $400K home, a 2% wind deductible is $8,000 out of pocket before the policy pays.

### How often should I review my coverage?

Every 12 months, and immediately after any major life change — renovations, a new roof, adding a pool, or a big furniture or electronics upgrade. Building costs have jumped sharply in Georgia over the last few years; many homes are underinsured by 20–30% without the owner realizing it.

### Are my belongings covered when I travel?

Yes — a typical HO-3 policy covers personal property anywhere in the world, usually up to 10% of your Coverage C limit. A laptop stolen from a hotel in Europe or a suitcase lost on a cruise is generally covered, subject to your deductible.

### What's the difference between market value and replacement cost?

Market value is what someone would pay for your home today, including the land. Replacement cost is what it would cost to rebuild the structure from scratch at today's labor and materials rates. Always insure to replacement cost — in many Georgia markets replacement cost is higher than market value.
